Aircon ROI — FAQ

Practical questions about sizing, system configurations, and using the calculator.

What's a "2-system" / "3-system" / "4-system"?

›

It's the SG installer shorthand for multi-split systems — one outdoor unit driving multiple indoor heads:

  • 1-system: 1 outdoor → 1 indoor (also called single-split)
  • 2-system: 1 outdoor → 2 indoor (typical: living + 1 bedroom)
  • 3-system: 1 outdoor → 3 indoor (living + master + 1 BR — the most common HDB configuration)
  • 4-system / 5-system: living + master + 2–3 BRs

Multi-split saves ledge space (one outdoor unit instead of N), but the published Weighted COP is for the matched system as a whole. In practice the real-world COP runs ~10% below an equivalent single-split because of part-load behavior and refrigerant losses across long pipe runs.

How many BTU do I need per room?

›

Singapore rule of thumb: ~600 BTU/h per m² of floor area (humidity adds latent load on top of pure heat removal). Quick table:

  • Standard bedroom (~10 m²) → 9,000 BTU = 2.6 kW
  • Master bedroom (~14 m²) → 12,000 BTU = 3.5 kW
  • Living/dining (~20 m²) → 18,000 BTU = 5.0 kW
  • Large open-plan living (~28 m²) → 24,000 BTU = 7.0 kW

West-facing rooms with afternoon sun benefit from one size up. Don't over-oversize — a too-large unit will cool fast then cycle without dehumidifying, leaving the room clammy.

Is upgrading from a 3★ to a 5★ aircon really worth it?

›

It depends entirely on hours of use. The calculator does this exact comparison — load it up with your rooms and hours.

Quick intuition: the energy ratio is COP_old / COP_new. A 2★ unit with COP ~3.4 vs a 5★ with COP ~5.5 means the new unit uses 3.4 / 5.5 = 62% of the energy for the same cooling. On a household pulling 2,500 kWh/year on aircon, that's ~950 kWh saved × current SG tariff = ~$320/year.

If the price premium is $1,500 (4★ → 5★ or 2★ → 5★), payback is ~5 years on the unit price difference alone. Faster if you're already replacing for end-of-life and don't count the full unit cost as marginal.

Where it doesn't pay back: a single bedroom run 2 hrs/night. Annual energy use is too low for the COP gap to matter much.

What's the multi-split COP penalty?

›

The COP value NEA publishes for a multi-split system is at matched test conditions with all indoor units operating. In real installs:

  • One outdoor unit serving multiple zones rarely runs all of them at design conditions simultaneously.
  • Long refrigerant pipe runs (HDB ledge → bedroom can be 8–12m) cost a few percent in heat-exchange efficiency.
  • When only one indoor head is on, the outdoor compressor often runs at minimum speed — less efficient than its sweet spot.

Net real-world COP typically falls 5–15% below rated. The calculator's default penalty is 10%, configurable in Tariffs & Assumptions.

What load factor and diversity factor should I use?

›

Load factor (α): how much of nameplate capacity the compressor actually delivers on average. An inverter unit cooling a moderately insulated room rarely runs at 100%; once at setpoint it idles down to 30–50%. Default 0.65 reflects that.

Diversity factor (δ): how much of total system capacity actually runs simultaneously. If your living room runs evenings and bedrooms at night, peak overlap is brief. Default 0.7.

If you tend to run all rooms at once (work-from-home + bedrooms + living all day), bump diversity to 0.85–1.0. If only one room at a time, drop it to 0.4–0.5.

What about the Climate Voucher / NEA rebates?

›

NEA and CDC schemes around energy-efficient appliances change frequently — rebate amounts, eligibility (HDB only / private / both), and which star ratings qualify all shift. Rather than hardcode rules that go stale, the calculator has a manual Rebate / Climate Voucher field on each option.

Check the current scheme directly:

  • NEA Energy Labelling Scheme
  • CDC Climate Voucher / Climate Action Pledge announcements (currently $300–400 for qualifying 4★/5★ aircons in many cycles).

Enter the actual rebate you'd receive. The calculator subtracts it from upfront cost when computing payback.

Why do the calculator's annual kWh numbers differ from NEA's?

›

NEA's published Annual Energy Consumption (kWh) uses a fixed standardised usage profile (specific outdoor temperature, fixed run hours per year) so all aircons can be compared apples-to-apples. It doesn't reflect your hours, room sizes, or load.

This calculator does the opposite: it computes energy use directly from the rooms you specify, your hours, and the rated Weighted COP/IEER. So if you run aircon more than NEA's assumed hours, your number will be higher; less, lower.

Treat the NEA kWh column on the Models page as a relative ranking (good for sorting), not an absolute prediction.

Should I pick a 3-phase VRV system?

›

Probably not, for typical HDB / smaller condo. VRV/VRF needs a 3-phase electrical supply, professional commissioning, and is much more expensive upfront. It shines when:

  • You have 6+ indoor heads (large landed, penthouse, two-storey).
  • Independent zone control with very different schedules matters (bedrooms vs living vs office vs guest room all run different hours).
  • You already have 3-phase supply (most HDB and many condos don't).

For a typical 3- or 4-system HDB/condo, a high-tick multi-split is almost always the better economic choice.
