How Aircons Work

What COP and IEER actually measure, why ticks matter, what an inverter does, and the difference between single-split, multi-split, and VRV systems.

TL;DR

  • COP = how many kWh of cooling you get per kWh of electricity. Higher is better. A 5★ aircon at COP 5.5 gives you 5.5 kWh of cooling for every 1 kWh you pay for.
  • Ticks are NEA's 1–5 star labels. Each tick is roughly +0.5–0.8 COP.
  • Inverter compressors modulate speed instead of cycling on/off — same cooling, less wasted energy, much quieter.
  • Multi-split shares one outdoor unit across rooms. Convenient, slightly less efficient than matching single-splits.
  • 5★ vs 3★ on a 3-system aircon running 6 hrs/day saves roughly $300–500/year in SG. Payback against the price premium is usually 4–6 years.

The cycle in one diagram

An aircon doesn't create cold — it moves heat from inside to outside, using a refrigerant that boils and condenses at convenient temperatures.

The compressor is the only major energy consumer. It spends 1 unit of electrical energy to pump 3–6 units of heat out of your home. That's why COP can exceed 1 (a heater can't — it converts electricity 1:1).

Higher COP = better refrigerant, larger heat exchangers, smarter controls.

COP, IEER, and Ticks

Full-load COP

Cooling ÷ Electricity at 100% rated capacity. Useful when the unit is hammering away at peak load (rare in practice).

Weighted / Part-load COP

A blended figure that gives more weight to part-load operation. Most relevant for residential use where the compressor cycles or modulates.

IEER (3-phase)

Same idea as Weighted COP, but explicitly weights performance at 25/50/75/100% load. Used for VRV/VRF and commercial systems.

Ticks (the green stars)

NEA's Energy Labelling Scheme rates aircons 1–5 ticks based on Weighted COP bands:

Ticks Weighted COP (typical) What it means
★~3.0Old non-inverter, basic units. Cheapest upfront, expensive to run.
★★~3.4Entry-level inverter. Common in older HDB units.
★★★—Unused in NEA's current scheme.
★★★★~4.7Mid-range modern inverter. Sweet spot for most renovations.
★★★★★~5.5Top-end inverter. Premium price, best running cost.

Inverter vs non-inverter

Non-inverter

  • Compressor runs full speed or off. Cycles repeatedly.
  • Each restart wastes energy and stresses the compressor.
  • Temperature swings ±2°C around the setpoint.
  • Cheaper to manufacture; cheaper upfront.
  • Most 1★/2★ units.

Inverter

  • Compressor speed varies smoothly with cooling demand.
  • Once at setpoint, runs at low power instead of stopping.
  • Tighter temperature control (±0.5°C); quieter.
  • 20–40% energy savings vs equivalent non-inverter at same hours.
  • All modern 4★/5★ units; most 2★ units.

Single-split, multi-split, and VRV

Single-split (1:1)

One outdoor unit per indoor unit. Highest published COP per pair, but you need ledge space for N outdoor units in an N-room home.

Typical for: 1-room studio, master-only setups, larger landed where each room has good outdoor access.

Multi-split (1 outdoor : N indoor)

One bigger outdoor unit drives 2–5 indoor heads — the typical SG "system" (2-system, 3-system, 4-system…). Single ledge footprint.

Real-world COP is usually ~10% lower than the single-split rating because of partial load and longer refrigerant runs.

VRV / VRF (3-phase)

Variable Refrigerant Volume systems. One large outdoor unit drives many indoor heads with independent control. Common in landed homes, penthouses, offices.

Rated by IEER instead of Weighted COP. Higher upfront, often higher install complexity, but very efficient at part load.
