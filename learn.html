How Water Heaters Work

A practical comparison of electric and gas water heating — what's happening inside, and what it costs you.

TL;DR

  • Electric heaters turn electricity directly into heat through a metal coil — very efficient (~97%), simple, slow to recover.
  • Gas heaters burn piped gas under a heat exchanger — less efficient (~82%), but gas is cheaper per kWh and they recover faster.
  • The raw tariff gap is ~20%, but after efficiency losses the real running-cost gap is closer to ~5%.
  • The bigger savings when switching to electric often come from dropping the monthly gas standing charge, not the per-kWh price.

Electric Water Heater

How it works

  1. Cold water enters the bottom of an insulated tank.
  2. An immersed heating element (a Nichrome resistance coil) is energised by mains AC.
  3. Electrical resistance in the coil dissipates as heat — almost all of it transfers to the surrounding water.
  4. A thermostat cycles the element on/off to hold the setpoint (typically 55–65°C).
  5. Hot water rises and is drawn from the top when a tap opens.

Key numbers

  • Efficiency: ~95–98% (resistance heating is nearly lossless)
  • Element power: 1.5–3 kW (storage), 8–30 kW (instant)
  • Recovery time (50L tank, ΔT 32°C): ~50–60 min on a 2 kW element
  • Lifespan: 8–12 years

Gas Water Heater

How it works

  1. Cold water enters the bottom of an insulated tank (or flows through coils, in tankless units).
  2. A gas burner below or beside a heat exchanger ignites piped gas (town gas, natural gas, or LPG).
  3. Hot combustion gases transfer heat to the water through a metal wall — the water and the flame never touch.
  4. Spent combustion gases (CO2, water vapour, residual heat) leave through a flue.
  5. A thermostat or flow sensor controls the gas valve to maintain temperature.

Key numbers

  • Efficiency: ~80–85% (15–20% lost up the flue as hot exhaust)
  • Burner output: 20–40 kW typical — far higher than electric elements
  • Recovery time: 2–5× faster than equivalent electric storage
  • Lifespan: 10–15 years (heat exchanger is the wear part)

The Real Economics

The headline "gas is 19.6% cheaper than electricity" comes from the raw tariff ratio ($0.2389 / $0.2972). But that compares the cost of a kWh you buy, not the cost of a kWh that ends up in your water. Once you account for efficiency losses, the gap shrinks dramatically.

Electric (97% efficient)

$0.2972 ÷ 0.97 = $0.306 / useful kWh

Gas (82% efficient)

$0.2389 ÷ 0.82 = $0.291 / useful kWh

Real running-cost gap: ~5%, not 20%. On 200 kWh/month of useful hot water that's about $3 / month — before even considering the gas standing charge.

The hidden saver: if you only have piped gas for the water heater, switching off the gas account saves the monthly standing charge (~$15–20). Over a year that's $180–240 — usually larger than the per-kWh savings either way.

When to Pick Which

Lean Electric if…

  • You're paying a gas standing charge for water heating only.
  • You have (or plan to add) solar PV.
  • You want a simpler install — no flue, no gas line.
  • Hot water demand is moderate and predictable.
  • You value safety: no combustion, no CO risk.

Lean Gas if…

  • You already have piped gas for the cooktop — standing charge is sunk cost.
  • Household demand is heavy and bursty (large family, back-to-back showers).
  • You need fast recovery / endless hot water.
  • You're in an older property with existing gas infrastructure.

Numbers above are typical-case estimates for the Singapore residential market. Actual figures vary with appliance model, usage pattern, climate, and tariff revisions. Cross-check current tariffs with SP Group, City Energy, and EMA.
