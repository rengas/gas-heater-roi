Refrigerator ROI — FAQ

Practical questions about whether (and when) to upgrade.

Is it really worth replacing my old fridge for a new energy-efficient one?

›

Usually not on energy savings alone. Run the calculator with your honest numbers and you'll typically see payback > 12 years against a normal $1,500 unit.

The case for replacement is almost always:

  • The compressor is failing or already failed.
  • Door seals can't hold temperature anymore.
  • Cooling has become uneven (food spoiling unexpectedly).
  • Repair cost approaches the new-unit cost.

When end-of-life forces your hand, then the calculator helps decide between 3★ and 4★ — and the energy gap often justifies the small price premium for 4★.

Why are there only 3★ and 4★ fridges in the registry — no 5★?

›

NEA's tick thresholds for fridges are tight, and 5★ requires efficiency levels that almost no current production model achieves while still meeting other consumer requirements (size, fast-cooling, freezer compartment volume).

It's not a flaw in the data — it's the actual market. A 4★ fridge is the practical "best you can buy" right now.

How accurate is the "Estimate from age + volume" mode?

›

It's a calibrated guess, not a measurement. The formula uses 0.85 kWh/L/yr as a baseline (slightly above the current 3★ median, charitable for old units when they were new) and degrades by 4% per year past age 5 — roughly matching seal-wear and compressor decline trends from utility studies.

For more accuracy, plug a smart plug or energy meter into the fridge for 3–7 days and extrapolate to a year. Use Custom mode with that number.

Big fudge factors: a poorly maintained 12-year-old can be worse than the estimate; a well-cared-for 15-year-old can be better. The estimate sits at a typical middle.

What size fridge do I actually need?

›

Rule of thumb is ~120 L per person, with adjustments for cooking style:

  • 1 person, eats out a lot → ~150 L
  • 2 people, mixed → ~300 L
  • 3–4 people, weekly grocery shop → ~450 L
  • 5+ people or batch cooking → ~600 L

Don't oversize — empty space still gets cooled. Don't undersize either: stuffing blocks airflow and the compressor works harder.

What about Climate Voucher / NEA rebates?

›

SG schemes around energy-efficient appliances change frequently — amounts and which star ratings qualify both shift. The calculator's Rebate field is manual; enter the actual dollar amount you'd receive (or 0 if none).

Check the current rules at NEA's site or the most recent CDC voucher cycle. As of recent cycles, qualifying 4★ fridges have attracted vouchers in the $150–300 range for HDB residents.

Why is the calculator's annual kWh sometimes different from NEA's published figure?

›

The only reason the calculator's number differs from the NEA label is the door-opening factor. Default is 1.0 (matches NEA's standard test). Bump it to 1.1 if you have a heavy household; drop to 0.9 if you barely open the fridge.

Beyond that, NEA's Annual kWh is realistic for SG kitchen conditions — fridges are tested at standardised 25°C ambient, close to a typical SG kitchen, so unlike aircon (where standard test ≠ tropical reality) the label number is trustworthy.

What's the door-opening factor?

›

A multiplier on annual kWh that captures real-world door-opening behaviour vs the NEA standard test. Each door opening lets warm air in and the compressor has to remove that heat.

  • 0.9 — single person, minimal cooking, fridge mostly stocked with drinks.
  • 1.0 — default. Standard household.
  • 1.1–1.15 — large family, heavy daily cooking, kids opening fridge constantly.

The effect on annual kWh is usually under ±15%. For most people, leaving it at 1.0 is fine.

Should I buy a bigger or smaller fridge to save energy?

›

Smaller — but only marginally, and only if you can actually live with the smaller volume. The energy difference between a 350L and 500L 4★ fridge is roughly 30 kWh/yr, or about $9/yr at SG tariffs. That's noise compared to the gap between 3★ and 4★ at the same volume (~70 kWh/yr).

The much bigger savings are in star rating, not size. Don't buy a too-small fridge to save energy and end up needing a chest freezer separately — that's worse on every dimension.
