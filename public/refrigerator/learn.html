How Fridges Work

What "Annual kWh" really measures, why ticks matter, what an inverter compressor changes, and how to size a fridge for a Singapore household.

TL;DR

  • A fridge moves heat from inside to your kitchen — same refrigeration cycle as an aircon, just colder.
  • Annual kWh on the NEA label is the realistic running cost — fridges run 24/7 at standardised ambient, close to SG kitchen reality.
  • Ticks: NEA's current scheme has only 3★ and 4★ for fridges. 5★ effectively doesn't exist on the registry.
  • Inverter compressors modulate speed instead of cycling on/off. Quieter, ~10–20% more efficient than fixed-speed.
  • Old fridge replacement rarely pays back on energy alone — it pays back when the old one fails.

The cycle in one diagram

A fridge is just an aircon running at a different setpoint. The compressor pumps refrigerant in a loop:

  1. Cold liquid evaporates inside the cabinet, absorbing heat from your food.
  2. Compressor squeezes it into a hot, high-pressure gas.
  3. Coils on the back / underneath dump that heat to the kitchen.
  4. The condensed liquid cycles back. Repeat.

The compressor is essentially the only meaningful energy consumer. A typical fridge today uses 0.6–0.9 kWh per litre of internal volume per year.

NEA Ticks for fridges

NEA's energy-efficiency thresholds are calibrated by volume so a 600L family fridge and a 200L studio fridge can both earn the same star count. The current registry has only 3★ and 4★ entries — other tick bands are either above the entire fleet or below the minimum threshold to even sell.

3★ (median ~0.81 kWh/L/yr)

The vast majority of new fridges on sale today (375 of 397 in the registry). Base efficient. Most major brands at most price points.

4★ (median ~0.62 kWh/L/yr)

~22 fridges. Premium inverter models. Roughly 25% less energy than 3★ for the same volume. Often $200–500 price premium.

For comparison: a typical 15-year-old fridge often runs at 1.0–1.5 kWh/L/yr — that's where the case for replacement gets stronger.

Inverter vs fixed-speed compressor

Fixed-speed

  • Compressor is either ON at full power or OFF.
  • Cycles repeatedly through the day (audible "kachunk").
  • Each restart wastes a slug of energy and stresses the motor.
  • Internal temperature swings ±2°C around setpoint.
  • Found in cheaper models and most pre-2015 fridges.

Inverter

  • Variable-speed compressor — runs slow most of the time.
  • Tighter ±0.5°C control; better food preservation.
  • Quieter (steady hum vs starts/stops).
  • ~10–20% more efficient at typical SG kitchen ambient.
  • Standard on most 4★ models; widespread on 3★ now.

Sizing for a Singapore household

A common rule of thumb is ~120 L per person, then add for cooking style:

Household Cooks at home rarely Cooks daily / has weekly grocery run
1 person~150 L~250 L
2 people~250 L~350 L
3–4 people~400 L~500 L
5+ people~500 L~600+ L

Oversizing wastes energy proportionally — empty space still gets cooled. Undersizing means stuffing the fridge so airflow is blocked, which makes the compressor work harder. Mostly, the energy difference between a 350L and 500L fridge is real but smaller than the difference between a 3★ and 4★ at the same volume.

Real-world energy tips

  • Set fridge to 4°C, freezer to −18°C — colder than necessary wastes 5–10% per °C extra.
  • Leave at least 5 cm clearance behind and above for the condenser to dump heat. Pushing it flush against a wall noticeably increases energy use.
  • Don't put it next to the oven or in direct afternoon sun — every °C of ambient adds ~3% energy.
  • Vacuum the back coils annually if accessible — dust acts as insulation in the wrong place.
  • Replace door seals when they no longer "snap" closed on a paper test — failed seals can add 20–30% to running cost.
  • A full fridge holds temperature better than an empty one, but that doesn't mean over-stuff: airflow matters.
