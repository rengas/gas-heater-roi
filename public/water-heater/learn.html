How Water Heaters Work

The three water heater types you'll see in Singapore homes — what's happening inside, what each costs to run, and when each makes sense.

TL;DR

  • Instant electric — compact wall unit, very efficient (~97%), no standby loss, but draws huge current (8–30 kW) and may need a dedicated circuit.
  • Electric storage tank — insulated tank with immersed element. Lower-power (1.5–3 kW), but pays standby loss 24/7 to keep the tank hot.
  • Gas (almost always instant in SG) — burner + heat exchanger, recovers fast, ~82% efficient (rest goes up the flue), gas is cheaper per kWh.
  • Raw tariff gap is ~20% gas vs electric, but after efficiency adjustment the real running-cost gap is closer to ~5%.
  • The hidden saver going electric: dropping the monthly gas standing charge, often $15–20 / month.

Instant Electric Heater

tankless · on-demand

How it works

  1. You open the hot tap — a flow sensor wakes the unit.
  2. Cold water passes through a small heating chamber with a high-power resistance coil.
  3. The coil dumps current as heat directly into the flowing water.
  4. An electronic regulator modulates power to deliver water at exactly the dial setpoint.
  5. Tap closes → flow stops → unit goes to standby (~0 W).

Key numbers

  • Efficiency: ~95–98% (resistance heating, no flue)
  • Element power: 8–30 kW — needs a dedicated high-amp circuit
  • Standby loss: essentially zero — no tank to keep hot
  • Recovery time: instant (water heats as it flows)
  • Lifespan: 8–12 years
  • Footprint: small, mounts in the bathroom near the showerhead

Electric Storage Tank

tank-based · pre-heated

How it works

  1. An insulated tank (typically 30–80 L) holds water pre-heated to a setpoint.
  2. An immersed Nichrome element heats the tank water; a thermostat cycles it on/off to hold ~60°C.
  3. You mix hot tank water with cold at the shower mixer to get ~40°C.
  4. As you draw hot water, cold inflow pushes it up; the element reheats whatever the thermostat detects.
  5. Even when nobody's home, the tank keeps cycling to fight heat loss to ambient — that's the standby loss.

Key numbers

  • Efficiency: ~95–98% during heating
  • Element power: 1.5–3 kW — runs on a normal circuit
  • Standby loss: ~25–30 kWh/month for a 50 L tank held at 60°C in 28°C ambient
  • Recovery time: ~50–60 min for a 50 L tank from cold (2 kW element)
  • Lifespan: 8–12 years (element + anode rod replaceable)
  • Footprint: larger; usually mounted above the shower or in a service yard

Gas Water Heater

typically instant in SG (Rinnai, Joven)

How it works

  1. Tap opens → flow sensor opens the gas valve and triggers electronic ignition.
  2. Cold water flows through a metal heat exchanger sitting above a gas burner.
  3. Combustion gases transfer heat to the water through the metal wall — flame and water never touch.
  4. A modulating valve adjusts gas flow so output temperature matches the dial setpoint.
  5. Spent combustion gases (CO2, water vapour, residual heat) leave through the flue — that's the ~15–20% efficiency loss.

Key numbers

  • Efficiency: ~80–85% (rest lost up the flue as hot exhaust)
  • Burner output: 20–40 kW typical — far higher than electric storage
  • Standby loss: zero (instant) / ~similar to electric tank if storage
  • Recovery time: instant — water heats as it flows
  • Lifespan: 10–15 years (heat exchanger is the wear part)
  • Install requirements: gas line + flue / ventilation; usually outdoor or service-yard mount

The Real Economics

The headline "gas is 19.6% cheaper than electricity" comes from the raw tariff ratio ($0.2389 / $0.2972). But that compares the cost of a kWh you buy, not the cost of a kWh that ends up in your water. After efficiency adjustment:

Instant electric (97%)

$0.2972 ÷ 0.97 = $0.306 / useful kWh

No standby. Costs only what you draw.

Electric tank (97% + standby)

$0.306 / kWh of draw, plus ~$8/mo standby (50 L)

Standby penalty hits regardless of usage.

Gas instant (82%)

$0.2389 ÷ 0.82 = $0.291 / useful kWh

Plus a $15–20 / month standing charge.

Real running-cost gap (instant electric vs gas instant): ~5%, not 20%. On 130 kWh/month of useful hot water that's about $2 / month. The gas standing charge ($15–20/mo) is usually larger than the per-kWh savings.

Where storage tanks lose money: a 50 L electric tank held at 60°C in 28°C ambient bleeds ~28 kWh/month as standby loss — about $8/month. That's nearly 100 kWh/year of pure waste, regardless of how many showers you actually take. Going from electric tank to instant (gas or electric) usually erases that.

When to Pick Which

Lean instant electric if…

  • The bathroom has (or can take) a high-amp dedicated circuit.
  • Hot water demand is light to moderate — 1–2 showers at a time.
  • You want zero standby loss & the smallest possible unit.
  • You want the simplest install — no flue, no gas, no tank to mount.
  • You're planning solar PV down the line.

Lean electric tank if…

  • Your wiring can't support a high-amp instant circuit.
  • You want bursty hot water on a normal 1.5–3 kW supply.
  • You shower at predictable times — the tank has time to recover.
  • You're replacing like-for-like and don't want to redo plumbing.
  • (Accept the standby loss as a cost of convenience.)

Lean gas if…

  • You already have piped gas for the cooktop — standing charge is sunk cost.
  • Heavy / bursty demand: large family, back-to-back showers.
  • You want endless hot water at full flow.
  • You're in an older property with existing flue / ventilation.
  • Bathroom can't take a high-amp electrical install.

Numbers above are typical-case estimates for the Singapore residential market. Actual figures vary with appliance model, usage pattern, climate, and tariff revisions. Cross-check current tariffs with SP Group, City Energy, and EMA.
